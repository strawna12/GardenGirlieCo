Topic 01

Pesticide-Free Pest Control

Because spraying chemicals in our warm, biodiverse Florida climate does more harm than good — and there are way better options.

Florida's warm, humid climate creates a paradise for pests — but it's also a paradise for the beneficial insects and organisms that naturally keep those pests in check. When we reach for pesticide sprays, we often kill both the bad guys and the good guys, creating a cycle of dependency.

The good news: there are effective, affordable alternatives that work with Florida's ecosystem instead of against it.

🌿 Florida Friendly Tip

The Florida-Friendly Landscaping™ program (UF/IFAS) recommends tolerating some pest damage — healthy plants can handle it, and the "pests" are often food for songbirds and beneficial insects.

🧴

Neem Oil

Cold-pressed neem oil disrupts pest life cycles without harming bees. Works on aphids, whitefly, scale, and fungal issues. Apply at dusk to protect pollinators.

🧼

Insecticidal Soap Spray

Mix 1 tbsp castile soap + 1 qt water. Kills soft-bodied pests on contact by disrupting their cell membranes. Totally safe for plants and people.

🌶️

Hot Pepper / Garlic Spray

Blend hot peppers or garlic with water, strain, and spray. Acts as a deterrent for chewing insects and squirrels. Reapply after rain.

🐞

Attract Beneficial Insects

Ladybugs, lacewings, and parasitic wasps naturally control aphids and caterpillars. Plant fennel, dill, and marigolds nearby to invite them in.

🌱

Diatomaceous Earth

This fossilized powder damages the exoskeletons of crawling insects. Great for slugs, ants, and roaches in garden beds. Use food-grade only.

Topic 02

Worm Composting (Vermicomposting)

Your kitchen scraps becoming garden gold — with a little help from some wiggly friends. Perfect for Florida apartments and small spaces!

Vermicomposting uses red wiggler worms (not regular earthworms!) to break down kitchen scraps into incredibly rich compost called "worm castings." In Florida's heat, it works fast — and it's one of the best amendments for our notoriously sandy soil.

A small worm bin can live under your kitchen sink, on a shaded patio, or in a closet. No smell when done correctly!

🌡️ Florida Heat Warning

Keep worm bins out of direct sun — worms die above 95°F. A shaded porch, garage, or interior is ideal in our summers. Insulate in rare freezes below 40°F.

1

Choose your bin. Start with a plastic storage bin (12–18 gallons). Drill small holes in the lid and sides for airflow, and holes in the bottom for drainage.

2

Add bedding. Shredded cardboard, newspaper, or coconut coir moistened to "wrung-out sponge" wetness. Fill ¾ of the bin.

3

Add your worms. Start with ½–1 lb of red wigglers. Source from local bait shops or order online. Never use yard earthworms!

4

Feed carefully. Add fruit & veggie scraps, coffee grounds, eggshells. Avoid meat, dairy, citrus in excess, and oily foods.

5

Harvest castings every 2–3 months. Push finished compost to one side, add fresh bedding to the other — worms will migrate. Use castings as soil amendment!

🪱 Worms eat half their weight daily
💦 Castings hold 3x more water than soil

Topic 03

Backyard Composting in Florida

Florida's heat is actually a composting superpower. Learn to turn yard and kitchen waste into black gold faster than anywhere else in the country.

Florida's warmth and moisture mean compost breaks down significantly faster than in northern states — a hot pile can be ready in as little as 4–6 weeks. The key is balancing "greens" (nitrogen) and "browns" (carbon) and keeping the pile moist.

Composting also helps with Florida's biggest challenge: building organic matter into our nutrient-poor sandy soil. Every batch you make is a soil amendment that money can't quite replace.

🟢

Greens (Nitrogen-rich)

Fruit & veggie scraps, coffee grounds, fresh grass clippings, plant trimmings, seaweed (great in coastal FL!)

🟤

Browns (Carbon-rich)

Dry leaves, cardboard, paper, straw, wood chips. Use palm fronds sparingly — they're slow to break down.

❌

Never Compost in FL

Meat, dairy, pet waste, diseased plants, or citrus in large amounts (attracts pests, slows decomp in our climate).

🌡️

The Magic Ratio

Aim for 3:1 browns to greens by volume. Turn pile every 1–2 weeks. It should feel like a wrung-out sponge — moist but not dripping.

🌴 Florida-Specific Tip

In summer, your pile may dry out fast. Cover it with cardboard or keep a lid on to retain moisture. In our rainy season, the pile may get too wet — add more browns and turn frequently.

Topic 04

Pollinator Garden Basics for Florida

Florida is home to 315+ native bee species plus monarchs, swallowtails, hummingbirds, and more. Your garden can be their sanctuary.

Florida's year-round mild climate means pollinators are active nearly every month. Unlike northern states, you can maintain a four-season pollinator garden without much effort. The key is providing food (nectar and pollen), shelter, and water year-round.

Even a single container garden with the right plants can become a meaningful habitat. Every flower counts — especially as natural Florida habitat continues to shrink.

🌎 Did You Know?

One-third of all food we eat depends on pollinators. Florida's commercial agriculture — including citrus, strawberries, and watermelon — relies heavily on bee pollination. Your backyard garden is part of a bigger picture.

Topic 05

Florida Native Plants — Why They Win

Native plants evolved here. They're drought-tolerant, pest-resistant, wildlife-friendly, and honestly some of the most beautiful plants you'll ever grow.

Florida natives have spent thousands of years adapting to our sandy soils, intense sun, summer deluges, and occasional cold snaps. Once established (usually after 1–2 growing seasons), most require little to no irrigation, fertilizer, or pest control — making them the ultimate low-maintenance choice.

They also support local wildlife in ways that non-native plants simply can't. Native trees, for example, support 35x more caterpillar species than non-natives — and caterpillars are critical food for baby birds.

💡 The Establishment Period

Even tough native plants need regular watering for the first 3–6 months while roots establish. After that, most can rely on Florida's natural rainfall alone.

🌿

Beautyberry (Callicarpa americana)

Stunning magenta berries in fall, feeds 40+ bird species. Grows in sun or shade, drought-tolerant once established.

🌾

Muhly Grass

Feathery pink plumes in fall, almost zero maintenance, great for borders and mass plantings.

🌲

Live Oak (Quercus virginiana)

Florida's great native tree. Supports over 500 species of caterpillars. Provides year-round shade and hurricane resistance.

🌼

Blanket Flower (Gaillardia)

Cheerful red-orange blooms all summer, incredibly drought-tolerant, beloved by butterflies.

🌸

Wild Azalea (Rhododendron)

Florida's native azalea blooms in early spring with fragrant pink flowers. Thrives in partial shade.

Topic 06

Mulching — Florida's Most Underrated Garden Move

A 3-inch layer of mulch can reduce watering needs by 50%, suppress weeds, and build the soil health Florida gardens desperately need.

In Florida's hot, sunny climate, bare soil heats up fast, moisture evaporates quickly, and weeds take over with enthusiasm. Mulch is the single most effective thing most Florida gardeners can do to improve plant health with minimal effort.

The best mulches for Florida actually break down and feed the soil over time — turning into the organic matter our sandy soil so desperately lacks.

Mulch Type Best For Florida Notes
Pine bark / nuggets Ornamental beds, trees Long-lasting, won't float away in rain Top Pick
Eucalyptus mulch All-purpose Florida-grown, decomposes well, lightweight
Leaves / pine straw Budget-friendly, native beds Free from your yard! Breaks down fast, builds soil
Wood chips (arborist) Deep-root trees, large areas Often free from tree services — great for paths Free!
Rubber mulch Playgrounds only Does NOT break down or feed soil — avoid for gardens Avoid

⚠️ Avoid "Volcano Mulching"

Never pile mulch against tree trunks (the "volcano" look). It causes rot, disease, and bark damage. Keep mulch 4–6 inches away from any trunk or stem. Pull it back like a donut, not a mound!

Topic 07

Rain Gardens & Rain Barrels

Florida gets 54 inches of rain a year — mostly in summer. Learn how to capture it, slow it down, and use it to your garden's advantage.

Florida has a feast-or-famine rain pattern: weeks of drought followed by daily afternoon thunderstorms. Most of that rain runs off into storm drains, carrying fertilizers, pesticides, and pollutants straight into our bays and waterways.

Rain gardens and rain barrels are elegant solutions: they capture and filter stormwater, reduce flooding, recharge groundwater, and give your plants access to free, unchlorinated water that they genuinely prefer.

🌊 For the Bay

In Tampa Bay area, reducing polluted runoff is one of the most impactful things a homeowner can do. Our local springs and estuaries have been significantly impacted by nutrient runoff from residential landscapes.

🪣

Rain Barrels — The Easy Win

Connect a barrel to your downspout. 55-gallon barrels can be found free or cheap through Pinellas and Hillsborough County programs. Use collected water during dry spells — plants love the unchlorinated rain water!

🏞️

Rain Gardens — How They Work

A shallow planted depression that collects runoff from rooftops or driveways. Water soaks in over 24–48 hrs instead of running off. Plant with flood-tolerant native plants like pickerelweed, blue flag iris, or cardinal flower.

📍

Placement Tips for FL

Site rain gardens at least 10 feet from foundations and 25 feet from septic systems. Florida's water table is high — test that water drains within 24 hours before planting.

🎁

Free Resources in FL

Pinellas County offers free rain barrel workshops and rebates. Check your county extension office or Florida-Friendly Landscaping™ program for local incentives!

Topic 08

Understanding Florida's Famously Difficult Soil

Florida's sandy soil drains fast and holds almost no nutrients — but once you understand it, you can absolutely work with it.

Most of Florida sits on ancient sandy substrate that drains quickly and holds very little organic matter or nutrients. This is why plants in Florida often look great initially (from nursery soil or added compost) then decline as roots hit native ground.

The solution isn't to fight Florida soil — it's to choose the right plants AND continually build organic matter through compost, mulch, and reduced tilling.

🔬

Get a Soil Test First

UF/IFAS Extension labs offer $7–$10 soil tests. Know your pH, nutrient levels, and organic matter before adding amendments. Most FL soil is pH 6–7, but coastal areas can be alkaline.

🌿

Add Compost — Always

Mix 2–4 inches of finished compost into planting beds annually. Compost improves water retention, adds nutrients, and feeds soil microorganisms that plants depend on.

🦠

Don't Over-Till

Tilling destroys the fungal networks (mycorrhizae) that help plant roots absorb water and nutrients. Use a broadfork or layer composting instead of rototilling.

🌊

Coastal Soil Cautions

Near the coast, soil can be highly alkaline with salt content. Choose salt-tolerant plants and consider raised beds with amended soil for vegetables and herbs.

🌡️ FL soil temp rarely drops below 60°F
🍄 Mycorrhizae are your plant's BFF

Topic 09

Florida's Two Garden Seasons (Not Four!)

Florida doesn't follow the spring/summer/fall/winter gardening calendar. Understanding our real seasons unlocks year-round success.

Florida essentially has two growing seasons: the warm/wet season (May–October) and the cool/dry season (November–April). What grows in one season often fails in the other — and that's completely normal!

Understanding this lets you stop trying to grow tomatoes in July (they hate FL summer) and start enjoying them in our gorgeous winter instead.

☀️

Warm Season (May–Oct): Tropical Lovers

Best for: sweet potato, okra, southern peas, herbs (basil, lemongrass), tropical flowers, bougainvillea, plumeria, hibiscus, and heat-tolerant annuals. High humidity and daily rain are your friends!

🍃

Cool Season (Nov–Apr): The Secret FL Garden

Best for: tomatoes, peppers, squash, greens, broccoli, strawberries, herbs (cilantro, parsley), and most annual flowers. Our "winter" is when FL gardening really shines!

🌴

Year-Round Performers

Native plants, established tropicals, palms, firebush, pentas, liriope, and most ornamental grasses look good in every season with minimal intervention.

❄️

Frost Prep for Central/North FL

Tampa Bay rarely freezes, but be ready! Have frost cloth on hand for sensitive tropicals. Bring potted plants inside. One frost can wipe out unprotected bougainvillea or impatiens overnight.

Topic 10

Water-Wise Gardening in Florida

Florida has a water crisis despite all that rain. These habits protect our aquifer and your water bill — and your plants will actually thank you for it.

Florida's Floridan Aquifer — the underground freshwater system that supplies drinking water to millions — is being depleted faster than it's being replenished. Outdoor irrigation accounts for up to 50% of household water use, much of it wasted through over-watering, evaporation, and runoff.

The great news: water-wise gardens often look better than thirsty ones, because overwatering is actually the #1 cause of plant death in Florida (not drought!). Less water, done correctly, grows stronger roots.

⏰

Water at the Right Time

Early morning (4–8am) is ideal. Water evaporates less, leaves dry before night (reducing fungal disease), and pressure is higher. Avoid evening watering in humid FL summers.

📏

Deep and Infrequent — Always

Water deeply 1–2x per week rather than shallowly every day. This trains roots to go deep, making plants dramatically more drought-tolerant. Shallow daily watering creates weak, surface-level roots.

💧

Drip Irrigation > Sprinklers

Drip irrigation delivers water directly to root zones, reducing waste by 30–50% vs. overhead sprinklers. Great for veggie gardens, container plants, and new plantings.

🌧️

Use a Rain Sensor

Florida law requires irrigation systems to have rain shutoff sensors — but many are broken or bypassed. A working rain sensor prevents irrigation from running during or after rain. Check yours!

🌵

Choose Drought-Tolerant Plants

Native plants, succulents like agave and aloe, ornamental grasses, and established tropicals can survive on Florida's rainfall alone once their roots are established. Design for resilience!

✅ FL Water Restriction Reminder

Most FL counties have year-round irrigation restrictions (typically 2 days/week by address). Check your county's current schedule — violations can result in fines. Restrictions are there to protect our shared water supply!
